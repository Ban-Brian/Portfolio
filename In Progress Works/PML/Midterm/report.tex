\subsection{Dataset and Subset}

\subsection{Preprocessing}

Every model is wrapped in a
\texttt{Pipeline([("scaler", StandardScaler()), ("clf", model)])} so that
pixel standardisation is computed \emph{inside} each cross-validation fold,
guaranteeing no data leakage:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{StandardScaler}: transforms each pixel dimension to zero mean and unit variance using statistics estimated on the training fold only.
  \item \textbf{Pipeline}: the scaler is never fit on held-out folds or the test set.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Data-Split Protocol}

We adopt \textbf{a single train/test split with 5-fold cross-validation for hyperparameter tuning}:

\begin{code}
# One stratified 80/20 split — test set is never touched during tuning
X_train_val, X_test, y_train_val, y_test = train_test_split(
    X, y, test_size=0.20, stratify=y, random_state=42)

# All hyperparameter selection uses only X_train_val
GridSearchCV(pipe, param_grid, cv=5,
             scoring='accuracy', n_jobs=-1)   # 5-fold, metric = accuracy

# Final evaluation: call .predict()/.predict_proba() on X_test once
\end{code}

\subsection{Multinomial Logistic Regression}

For $K$ classes the model defines:
\[
P(Y_i = k \mid X_i = x_i)
  = \frac{\exp\!\bigl(\alpha_k + x_i^\top \beta_k\bigr)}
         {\displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{K} \exp\!\bigl(\alpha_j + x_i^\top \beta_j\bigr)},
  \qquad k = 1,\ldots,K,
\]
where $\alpha_k \in \mathbb{R}$ is a class-specific intercept and
$\beta_k \in \mathbb{R}^p$ is a class-specific weight vector.
This is a \textbf{probabilistic classifier} because:
(a)~the softmax normalisation guarantees non-negative outputs summing to~1;
(b)~it directly models $P(Y=k\mid X)$, the posterior class probability;
(c)~the spread of the probability vector quantifies prediction uncertainty.

\textbf{Prediction rule:}
$\hat{y}_i = \arg\max_{k}\; P(Y_i = k \mid x_i)$
--- assign the class with the highest fitted posterior probability.

\section{Part B\,: Frequentist Classification and Regularization}

\subsection{Models and Penalty Formulations}

Six models are fit, all using the above Pipeline pattern:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Logistic Regression -- No Penalty}: $C=\infty$ (\texttt{lbfgs},
        unregularised baseline)
  \item \textbf{Ridge LR} ($\ell_2$): minimises
        $\mathcal{L}(\beta)+\lambda\|\beta\|_2^2$, $\lambda = 1/C$
  \item \textbf{Lasso LR} ($\ell_1$): minimises
        $\mathcal{L}(\beta)+\lambda\|\beta\|_1$ (\texttt{saga} solver)
  \item \textbf{Elastic Net LR}: $\mathcal{L}(\beta)+\lambda\bigl[\rho\|\beta\|_1
        +\tfrac{1-\rho}{2}\|\beta\|_2^2\bigr]$
  \item \textbf{Linear SVM} (\texttt{LinearSVC}): maximises the hard/soft margin;
        $C$ controls the penalty on margin violations
  \item \textbf{RBF SVM} (extension): non-linear kernel $k(x,x')=\exp(-\gamma\|x-x'\|^2)$
\end{enumerate}

Ridge shrinks coefficients uniformly but never to exactly zero.
Lasso promotes exact sparsity via the $\ell_1$ geometry.
Elastic Net blends both behaviours through the mixing ratio $\rho\in(0,1)$.

\subsection{Frequentist--Bayesian Correspondence}

The MAP estimate under prior $\pi(\beta)$ equals the penalised MLE solution:
\[
\hat\beta_{\mathrm{MAP}} = \arg\max_\beta \bigl[\log p(y\mid X,\beta) + \log\pi(\beta)\bigr].
\]

\begin{center}\small
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.3}
\begin{tabular}{@{}llll@{}}
\toprule
Frequentist penalty & Bayesian prior & Correspondence & Shrinkage character \\
\midrule
Ridge ($\lambda\|\beta\|_2^2$)     & $\beta_j\!\sim\!\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2)$, $\sigma^2\!=\!\tfrac{1}{2\lambda}$ & MAP $=$ Ridge & Uniform, smooth \\
Lasso ($\lambda\|\beta\|_1$)       & $\beta_j\!\sim\!\mathrm{Laplace}(0,b)$, $b\!=\!\tfrac{1}{2\lambda}$         & MAP $=$ Lasso & Sparse, sharp \\
--- (extension)                     & Horseshoe ($\beta_j=z_j\lambda_j\tau$)                                       & Adaptive MAP & Spike-and-slab \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The crucial distinction is that the Bayesian approach provides a \emph{full posterior
distribution} $p(\beta\mid\text{data})$, not just a point estimate---enabling
principled predictive uncertainty quantification.

\subsection{Model Specification (PyMC, NUTS)}

All three models share the likelihood:
$Y_i\mid p_i \sim \mathrm{Bernoulli}(p_i)$,\;
$p_i = \sigma\!\bigl(\text{intercept} + x_i^\top\beta\bigr)$,\;
$\text{intercept}\sim\mathcal{N}(0,10)$.
They differ only in the prior on $\beta$:

\paragraph{(i) Gaussian Prior (Ridge analogue)}
\[
\beta_q \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\,1), \quad q=1,\ldots,30.
\]

\paragraph{(ii) Laplace Prior (Lasso analogue)}
\[
\beta_q \sim \mathrm{Laplace}(0,\,1), \quad q=1,\ldots,30.
\]

\paragraph{(iii) Horseshoe Prior (Extension --- non-centred form)}
\[
\tau \sim \mathrm{HalfCauchy}(1),\quad
\lambda_q \sim \mathrm{HalfCauchy}(1),\quad
z_q \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1),\quad
\beta_q = z_q\cdot\lambda_q\cdot\tau.
\]
The non-centred reparameterisation ($\beta$ as a deterministic transform of
the auxiliary $z$) substantially reduces NUTS divergences compared to the
centred form.

\textbf{Sampling:} 1{,}000 draws, 500 warm-up steps, 2 chains
(\texttt{target\_accept=0.9} for Horseshoe).

\subsection{Posterior Predictive Probabilities}

We compute the \emph{true posterior predictive} probability, \textbf{not} the
plug-in approximation $\sigma(\mathbb{E}[\eta_i])$:
\[
\hat p_i
 = \mathbb{E}\!\bigl[\sigma(\eta_i)\mid\text{data}\bigr]
 = \frac{1}{S}\sum_{s=1}^{S}
    \sigma\!\bigl(\text{intercept}^{(s)} + x_i^\top\beta^{(s)}\bigr),
\qquad S = 2{,}000 \text{ samples.}
\]
The two differ whenever the posterior over $\beta$ has appreciable spread;
the correct form always lies closer to 0.5 (more uncertain) than the plug-in.
Predictive uncertainty is:
\[
\hat\sigma_i = \mathrm{std}_{s}\!\bigl[\sigma(\eta_i^{(s)})\bigr].
\]

\section{Part D\,: Compare and Contrast}
%=================================================================

\subsection{Which Model Performed Best as a Classifier?}

On the 10-class MNIST test set, \textbf{RBF SVM} achieves the highest test
accuracy ($\approx$96.1\%), followed by Linear SVM ($\approx$92.2\%) and
Ridge LR ($\approx$92.1\%).
The unpenalised logistic baseline trails at $\approx$88.6\%---a drop of more
than 3~percentage points---confirming that regularisation is not optional at
$p/n = 784/4000 \approx 0.2$.

\subsection{Did the Best Classifier Provide the Best Probabilistic Fit?}

\textbf{No.}  RBF SVM yields no calibrated probabilities and therefore no
log-loss.  Among models that do produce probabilities, \textbf{Ridge LR}
achieves the lowest log-loss (best probabilistic calibration).
This is expected: multinomial logistic regression maximises the conditional
log-likelihood directly, so minimising log-loss is its \emph{training
objective}; SVM optimises the (surrogate) hinge loss with no connection to
probability calibration.

\subsection{Ridge vs.\ Lasso: Shrinkage, Sparsity, and Interpretability}

\begin{center}\small
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.2}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
Aspect & Ridge ($\ell_2$) & Lasso ($\ell_1$) \\
\midrule
Sparsity      & None (all non-zero)       & $\sim$70\% exactly zero \\
Stability     & High (handles correlated features) & Low with correlated predictors \\
Interpretability & Coefficient magnitudes graded & Binary: in/out of model \\

\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

On MNIST the pixel features are strongly correlated (adjacent pixels; digit
strokes are spatially smooth), which favours Ridge.
Lasso's exact-zero behaviour is still \emph{interpretable}---the remaining
non-zero pixels directly identify stroke regions---but the accuracy cost is
real.
Elastic Net ($\approx$40\% sparsity, test acc.\ 0.917) provides a useful
middle ground, recovering most of Ridge's accuracy while yielding sparser maps.

\subsection{SVM vs.\ Multinomial Logistic Regression}

\begin{center}\small
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.2}
\begin{tabular}{@{}p{3.3cm}p{5cm}p{5cm}@{}}
\toprule
Aspect & Logistic Regression & SVM \\
\midrule
Training objective & Conditional log-likelihood (probabilistic) & Margin maximisation (geometric) \\
Output            & Calibrated probabilities $\hat p_k$ & Hard labels (probability requires Platt scaling) \\
Loss function     & Cross-entropy / log-loss & Hinge loss \\
Which samples matter & All, weighted by residuals & Support vectors only \\
Regularisation    & $\ell_1$, $\ell_2$, or Elastic Net on $\beta$ & $C$ trades off margin vs.\ violations \\
Interpretability  & Coefficients have direct probabilistic meaning & Weights explain margin geometry only \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

SVM's geometric margin criterion is inherently robust to noise far from the
boundary, which---combined with the RBF kernel's implicit infinite-dimensional
feature map---explains its superior hard-label accuracy on MNIST.
Logistic regression's probabilistic output is indispensable when prediction
confidence feeds downstream decisions (e.g., a model flagging ambiguous digits
for human review).

\subsection{Gaussian vs.\ Laplace Priors as Bayesian Analogues}

At the \emph{MAP level}, Bayesian-with-Gaussian $\equiv$ Ridge and
Bayesian-with-Laplace $\equiv$ Lasso.
The Bayesian framework adds three things the frequentist penalties cannot:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Full posterior}: credible intervals on each $\beta_q$, not just
        a point estimate.  The Gaussian posterior (approximately Gaussian via
        the Laplace approximation) gives symmetric, smooth credible intervals;
        the Laplace posterior can concentrate mass at zero.
  \item \textbf{Predictive uncertainty}: $\hat\sigma_i$ measures sample-level
        ambiguity---directly actionable for active learning, flagging difficult
        examples, or communicating risk.
  \item \textbf{Adaptive shrinkage (Horseshoe)}: neither Ridge nor Lasso adapts
        the penalty strength per-coefficient.  The Horseshoe does, leaving
        dominant PCA components nearly unpunished while aggressively shrinking
        noise components.  This matches the geometry of the 3-vs-8 task, where
        a handful of PCA directions carry most of the signal.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{The Role of Regularisation in Classification}

Regularisation serves three complementary functions:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Variance reduction}: shrinking $\|\beta\|$ prevents overfitting,
        improving test-set generalisation.  Here, Ridge and Lasso both outperform
        unpenalised logistic regression by $\ge 2$ percentage points on a
        784-dimensional feature space with 4{,}000 training samples.
  \item \textbf{Feature selection}: $\ell_1$ penalties (Lasso, Horseshoe) identify
        which pixel directions or PCA components are genuinely discriminative,
        reducing both storage and computation in downstream applications.
  \item \textbf{Prior belief encoding}: in the Bayesian view regularisation is not
        an ad-hoc penalty but a formal statement about plausible coefficient
        magnitudes \emph{before} observing data.  This framing makes the choice
        of regulariser a modelling decision, not a tuning knob---and opens the
        door to data-driven hyperpriors (e.g., the global scale $\tau$ in the
        Horseshoe).
\end{enumerate}

The \emph{form} of regularisation matters as much as its strength.
RBF SVM achieves the best accuracy not via stronger penalisation but by
expanding the hypothesis class through an implicit infinite-dimensional feature
map, capturing non-linear structure that no linear model can represent.
Ridge LR achieves the best calibration because its objective is calibration.
Bayesian methods provide full predictive distributions that neither the SVM
nor any frequentist logistic model can match.
